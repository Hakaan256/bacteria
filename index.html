I don't think our model seems very realistic. How can we make our model more realistic?
Good point. We'll add the concepts of inheritance and mutation to our model. Every trait, or characteristic, of a bacterium (such as its shape, size, and behavior) is encoded in its genetic material - its DNA. Most bacteria reproduce simply by dividing in two, with copies of its DNA in each new cell. Sometimes the process of DNA replication doesn't make a perfect copy, and the DNA is not quite identical. This results in differences between the new bacterium's traits and those of the parent - sometimes small, and sometimes drastic. These differences are called mutations.
What will that look like in our model?
Hmm... what trait can we use to model how mutations occur and are inherited? Aha! We can use a slight color difference to model a mutation. In our model, we'll let some bacteria mutate by changing color value - the lightness or darkness of the color. We'll allow the new color to be inherited during reproduction.



Biodiversity!
Our new model is based on these key ideas:

- Bacteria sometimes reproduce, dividing to make more.

- Bacteria eventually die.

- Bacteria offspring inherit the traits of the parent.

- Mutations in bacteria DNA can occur.

- Mutations can be inherited by a bacterium's offspring.
Notice how, in our model, mutations lead to small communities of bacteria similar in color value - this looks like lighter patches and darker patches.
Wait, how is this related to antibiotic resistance?
Antibiotics are substances known to have bacterium-killing properties. A bacterium develops antibiotic resistance when mutations combine to make it hard to kill. When a bacterium has antibiotic resistance, it is more likely to survive even when antibiotic substances are present.

Antibiotic resistance is an inheritable trait. If a bacterium cannot survive, it cannot reproduce. And if a bacterium has antibiotic resistance, it can survive to reproduce, passing this trait on to its offspring! Of course, not all of the new bacteria will have antibiotic resistance. Some will have additional mutations that weaken resistance. What do you think will happen to these weaker bacteria when additional doses of antibiotic are applied?

So, what's happening here? Why are the bacteria getting stronger in some cases, and being totally eliminated in others?
When you apply an antibiotic, you're preventing the reproduction of any bacteria that are killed. Because bacteria with less antibiotic resistance are the most likely to die, the only ones left to reproduce (and pass on their traits) are the strong ones—the ones with the most antibiotic resistance!

Umm... How accurate was our model?
That is an excellent question! Always be cautious to accept conclusions drawn from simplified models. Simple models are useful in exploring an idea to gain a more intuitive understanding- but they're no replacement for the scientific method!

Some key gaps in our model:
- In reality, colonies of bacteria are MUCH bigger than in our model. At any given time, our bodies hold trillions of individual bacterium!
- In reality, the mutation rate is MUCH slower. DNA is incredibly complex. For a random error in its duplication to result in anything useful, a lot of lucky factors need to fall into place.
- "Super unkillable bacteria" aren't created in a single body. Because useful mutations occur so rarely, the process of developing such strong resistance happens over many, many generations, through infections in many different individuals.
- Our model didn't take into account many other factors at play in a bacterial infection, such as the body's natural defenses against infection, the presence of "good bacteria", and so on.
So, should I use antibiotics or not?
Another excellent question - one that can only really be answered by a doctor. But, here are some of the issues a doctor will consider when deciding whether to prescribe antibiotics and how much antibiotic is needed.
Some diseases are caused by viruses, fungi, parasites, and other infectious pathogens. That means that antibiotics - which only kill bacteria - will do nothing to help!
The human body naturally has strong defenses against infection. In most cases, the added help of antibiotics is unneeded.
In the case that your body does need help, the dosage needs to be considered very carefully.
Why?
Well, we saw that there is a danger in killing most of the bacteria but not all of them.
Does this mean we should take a very large amount of antibiotics whenever we might need it?
No! Our bodies contain mostly good bacteria - bacteria we need for digestion and other bodily functions. Unfortunately, antibiotics kill all kinds of bacteria - they don't care about which ones help and which ones hurt!
On the other hand, if we take too low a dose of an antibiotic, we risk leaving only the strongest to further infect our bodies and the other people around us.
This is the tricky balance best left to doctors. If there's one thing you can do - it's listen to them! If they prescribe a week's worth of pills, make sure you take them for the whole week, even if you're feeling better! You might start to feel better, but the strongest of the bacteria could still be making their last stand.
